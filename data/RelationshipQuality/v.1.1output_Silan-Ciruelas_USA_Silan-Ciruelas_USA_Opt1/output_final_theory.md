**Core Category (Selective Code):  
“Dynamic Co‑Construction of Marital Stability through Trust, Communication, and Role Negotiation.”**

**Grounded‑Theory Explanation**  
Across the data set, participants describe marriage as an ongoing, negotiated process in which partners continuously build and rebuild a shared sense of safety and satisfaction. The foundation of this process is **mutual trust and open communication**, which enable couples to disclose needs, apologize, and provide emotional and practical support. When disagreements arise, couples employ a spectrum of **conflict‑management strategies**—from deliberate silence and delayed discussion to the enlistment of third‑party mediators—aimed at preserving relational harmony without eroding trust. Simultaneously, the **division of parenting duties, household chores, and sexual/affectional intimacy** forces partners to renegotiate their roles, often prompting personal identity shifts (e.g., from student to parent) and requiring adaptive skill‑learning (cooking, caregiving). These role negotiations are further shaped by **external social and cultural pressures** (family expectations, peer divorce narratives) and by the **structural context of the household** (nuclear vs. extended family). Positive outcomes—high satisfaction ratings, feelings of safety, and perceived stability—emerge when partners successfully integrate trust, responsive support, and flexible role negotiation; breakdowns in any of these dimensions tend to generate conflict, dissatisfaction, or perceived instability. Thus, marital quality is understood as a dynamic co‑construction in which trust, communication, and negotiated roles interact continuously to produce—or undermine—relationship stability.
